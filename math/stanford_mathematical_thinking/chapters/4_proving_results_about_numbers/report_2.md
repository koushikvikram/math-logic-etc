## Part 2: Quiz Answer Key

1. **What is the primary distinction between elementary arithmetic and number theory?**
   Elementary arithmetic focuses on mere calculation and practical use of numbers for counting. In contrast, number theory is the abstract study of the integers and the properties they exhibit within their arithmetical system.

2. **State the Division Theorem (Theorem 4.1.1) and identify its components.**
   The theorem states that for any integers $a$ and $b$ (where $b > 0$), there exist unique integers $q$ and $r$ such that $a = q \cdot b + r$ and $0 \le r < b$. In this equation, $q$ is the quotient and $r$ is the remainder.

3. **What does the notation $b|a$ signify, and how does it differ from $a/b$?**
   The notation $b|a$ indicates a relationship of divisibility, meaning $a$ is divisible by $b$ if there is an integer $q$ such that $a = b \cdot q$. While $b|a$ is a statement that is either true or false, $a/b$ is a notation representing a specific number.

4. **What was the pedagogical purpose of David Hilbert’s "Hilbert’s Hotel" story?**
   Hilbert used the hypothetical infinite hotel to illustrate the surprising and counter-intuitive properties of infinity, such as the ability to accommodate new guests in a full hotel. It demonstrates that while intuition may fail with infinity, rigorous mathematical procedures can describe how infinitely many steps are carried out.

5. **How does the text define a prime number versus a composite number?**
   A prime number is defined as an integer $p > 1$ that is only divisible by 1 and itself. A natural number $n > 1$ that does not meet this criteria and is not prime is categorized as a composite number.

6. **State the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic regarding the decomposition of natural numbers.**
   The theorem states that every natural number greater than 1 is either a prime or can be expressed as a product of prime numbers. This prime decomposition is unique, regardless of the order in which the primes are written.

7. **In the context of the rational numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$), what is meant by the property of "density"?**
   Density refers to the property where, between any two unequal rational numbers $r$ and $s$, there is always another rational number $t$. This suggests that rational numbers are packed closely together, though they still contain "holes" that are filled by irrational numbers.

8. **Explain the difference between an open interval $(a, b)$ and a closed interval $[a, b]$.**
   An open interval $(a, b)$ is a set of real numbers that begins just after $a$ and ends just before $b$, excluding the endpoints themselves. A closed interval $[a, b]$ includes both $a$ and $b$ as members of the set.

9. **Define the Completeness Property of the real number system.**
   The Completeness Property states that any non-empty set of real numbers that possesses an upper bound must also have a least upper bound within the real numbers. This property is what distinguishes the real numbers from the rational numbers, which contain "holes."

10. **In formal terms, what does it mean to say that a sequence $\{a_n\}$ tends to a limit $a$?**
    It means that for every positive real number $\epsilon$, there exists a natural number $n$ such that for all terms $m \ge n$, the absolute difference $|a_m - a|$ is less than $\epsilon$. Essentially, the terms of the sequence eventually stay within an arbitrarily small distance of the limit $a$.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Absolute Value** | Denoted as $|a|$, it is the number resulting from removing any minus sign; formally, $|a| = a$ if $a \ge 0$ and $|a| = -a$ if $a < 0$. |
| **Archimedean Property** | The property stating that for any positive real numbers $x$ and $y$, there exists a natural number $n$ such that $nx > y$. |
| **Completeness Property** | The principle that every non-empty set of real numbers that is bounded above has a least upper bound in $\mathbb{R}$. |
| **Composite Number** | A natural number $n > 1$ that is not a prime number. |
| **Density** | The property of rational numbers where between any two unequal rationals, there exists a third rational number. |
| **Divisibility ($b|a$)** | A relationship where an integer $a$ is divisible by a non-zero integer $b$ if there exists an integer $q$ such that $a = b \cdot q$. |
| **Greatest Lower Bound (glb)** | A number that is a lower bound of a set and is greater than or equal to any other lower bound of that set. |
| **Integers ($\mathbb{Z}$)** | The set of whole numbers, including positive, negative, and zero (from the German *Zahlen*). |
| **Interval** | An uninterrupted stretch of the real line; can be open, closed, or half-open. |
| **Least Upper Bound (lub)** | Also called the supremum; the smallest value that is greater than or equal to every element in a set. |
| **Limit** | A fixed number $a$ that the terms of a sequence $a_n$ approach arbitrarily closely as $n$ increases. |
| **Number Theory** | The branch of pure mathematics dedicated to the study of the integers and their abstract properties. |
| **Prime Number** | An integer $p > 1$ whose only divisors are 1 and itself. |
| **Quotient** | The integer $q$ resulting from the Division Theorem, representing how many times the divisor fits into the total. |
| **Rational Numbers ($\mathbb{Q}$)** | Numbers that can be expressed as a ratio of two integers $a/b$, where $b \ne 0$. |
| **Real Numbers ($\mathbb{R}$)** | The set encompassing both rational and irrational numbers, representing a continuous line without "holes." |
| **Remainder** | The integer $r$ in the Division Theorem that satisfies $0 \le r < |b|$, representing the "leftover" value after division. |
| **Sequence** | A collection of real numbers $a_n$ associated with each natural number $n$ and arranged in a specific order. |
| **Upper Bound** | A number $b$ is an upper bound of a set $A$ if every element $a$ in $A$ is less than or equal to $b$. |
