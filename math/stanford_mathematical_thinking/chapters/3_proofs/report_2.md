## Part 1: Short-Answer Quiz

**Instructions:** Answer the following questions in two to three sentences based on the information provided in the source context.

1.  **How does the establishment of truth differ between the natural sciences and mathematics?**
    In the natural sciences, truth is established through empirical means such as observation, measurement, and experimentation. In contrast, mathematical truth is determined by constructing a logically sound argument, known as a proof, which establishes the truth of a statement.

2.  **What are the two primary purposes for constructing a mathematical proof?**
    Proofs are created to establish truth and to communicate that truth to others. A proof must be logically sound to convince the author of a statement’s validity, and it must be written clearly enough for the intended audience to follow and understand the explanation.

3.  **Why is the Goldbach Conjecture currently not considered a theorem by mathematicians?**
    Although the Goldbach Conjecture has been verified by computer programs for numbers up to 1.6 quintillion, it has not yet been proven through a logically sound argument. In mathematics, a theorem requires a formal proof, whereas a conjecture is a statement that is believed to be true but remains unproven.

4.  **What is required to disprove a mathematical conjecture like the Goldbach Conjecture?**
    To disprove the conjecture, one would only need to find a single counterexample. In the case of Goldbach’s statement, finding just one even number greater than 2 that cannot be expressed as the sum of two primes would be sufficient to invalidate the entire claim.

5.  **Describe the core strategy behind a "proof by contradiction."**
    The strategy involves assuming the negation of the statement one wishes to prove and then reasoning until an obviously false or contradictory conclusion is reached. Because a true assumption cannot logically lead to a false consequence, the original assumption must be false, thereby proving the original statement is true.

6.  **Why is Andrew Wiles' proof of Fermat’s Last Theorem significant to the mathematical community?**
    Wiles' proof is significant because it finally resolved a conjecture that had remained unproven since the seventeenth century. By providing a deep, logically sound argument that convinced experts in the field, he transformed Fermat’s long-standing conjecture into a recognized theorem.

7.  **What is the difference between using a "random" number and an "arbitrary" number in a universal proof?**
    A random number is a specific instance that might happen to satisfy a property, which does not prove the statement for all cases. An "arbitrary" number is a symbol (like $n$) used to represent any possible value in a set without restriction, ensuring the logic of the proof applies universally.

8.  **How does a mathematician typically prove a biconditional statement ($\phi \Leftrightarrow \psi$)?**
    A biconditional is usually proven by establishing two separate conditionals: $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$ and $\psi \Rightarrow \phi$. Alternatively, one can prove the two conditionals $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$ and $(\neg \phi) \Rightarrow (\neg \psi)$ to establish the same equivalence.

9.  **What are the two essential steps required in a proof by mathematical induction?**
    The first step is the "Initial step," where the statement is verified for the first natural number, typically $n = 1$. The second step is the "Induction step," where one proves the conditional that if the statement holds for an arbitrary $n$, it must also hold for $n + 1$.

10. **What historical significance is associated with the proof that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational?**
    The discovery caused a crisis in ancient Greek mathematics because it revealed geometric lengths that could not be measured by their existing number system. This problem was not fully resolved until the nineteenth century with the development of the real number system.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Biconditional** | A statement of the form "$\phi$ if and only if $\psi$," requiring both $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$ and $\psi \Rightarrow \phi$ to be true. |
| **Composite Number** | A natural number that is not prime; it can be expressed as a product of smaller natural numbers. |
| **Conditional** | A statement of the form "If $\phi$, then $\psi$" (denoted $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$). |
| **Conjecture** | A mathematical statement that is suspected to be true but has not yet been proven. |
| **Contrapositive** | The logically equivalent form of a conditional $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$, expressed as $\neg \psi \Rightarrow \neg \phi$. |
| **Counterargument** | An objection or flaw that a reader might use to challenge the validity of a proof. |
| **Existence Statement** | A claim that at least one object exists satisfying a certain property (denoted $\exists x A(x)$). |
| **Induction (Mathematical)** | A method of proof used for statements about natural numbers involving an initial case and an induction step to show the statement holds for all $n$. |
| **Irrational Number** | A number that cannot be expressed as a ratio of two natural numbers (e.g., $\sqrt{2}$). |
| **Modus Ponens** | A rule of logic stating that if $\phi$ is true and $\phi \Rightarrow \psi$ is true, then $\psi$ must be true. |
| **Prime Number** | A natural number greater than 1 that cannot be formed by multiplying two smaller natural numbers. |
| **Proof** | A logically sound argument that establishes the truth of a mathematical statement. |
| **Proof by Cases** | A proof technique that establishes a result by showing it holds true in every possible scenario or "case." |
| **Proof by Contradiction** | A proof strategy that assumes the negation of a statement to derive a logical impossibility, thereby proving the original statement. |
| **Rational Number** | A number that can be expressed as a fraction $p/q$, where $p$ and $q$ are natural numbers. |
| **Theorem** | A mathematical statement that has been established as true through a formal proof. |
| **Universal Statement** | A claim that a certain property holds for all members of a given set (denoted $\forall x A(x)$). |
